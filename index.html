Diabetes Analysis

Diabetes is a chronic condition that affects how your body turns food into energy. There are several types of diabetes: Type 1, Type 2, and gestational diabetes.

Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes is thought to be caused by an autoimmune reaction (the body attacks itself by mistake) that stops your body from making insulin. Approximately 5-10% of the people who have diabetes have type 1.

Type 2 Diabetes

With type 2 diabetes, your body doesn’t use insulin well and can’t keep blood sugar at normal levels. About 90-95% of people with diabetes have type 2.

Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes develops in pregnant women who have never had diabetes. If you have gestational diabetes, your baby could be at higher risk for health problems.

    • Medical Based Analysis

    Mediacal requirement

    Insulin: Essential for managing Type 1 diabetes as the body cannot produce insulin. Various types include: Rapid-acting insulin (e.g., Lispro, Aspart) Short-acting insulin (e.g., Regular insulin) Intermediate-acting insulin (e.g., NPH) Long-acting insulin (e.g., Glargine, Detemir) Ultra-long-acting insulin (e.g., Degludec)

    Metformin: Often the first medication prescribed. It helps reduce glucose production in the liver. Sulfonylureas: Increase insulin production by the pancreas (e.g., Glipizide, Glyburide). Meglitinides: Stimulate the pancreas to release more insulin (e.g., Repaglinide, Nateglinide). Thiazolidinediones: Improve insulin sensitivity (e.g., Pioglitazone, Rosiglitazone). DPP-4 Inhibitors: Help reduce blood sugar levels without causing hypoglycemia (e.g., Sitagliptin, Saxagliptin).

    GLP-1 Receptor Agonists: Slow digestion and help lower blood sugar levels (e.g., Exenatide, Liraglutide). SGLT2 Inhibitors: Help the kidneys remove glucose from the bloodstream (e.g., Canagliflozin, Dapagliflozin). Insulin: May be required if other medications are not effective.

    What is BMI ?

    Body mass index (BMI) is a value derived from the mass (weight) and height of a person.

    The BMI is defined as the body mass divided by the square of the body height, and is expressed in units of kg/m2, resulting from mass in kilograms (kg) and height in metres (m).

    In comparison with the non-diabetic patient group, the average plasma glucose (mean) in diabetic patients was higher. Similar to this, individuals with diabetes had salivary glucose (mean) reported higher compared to non-diabetic patients (4.61 ± 2.58 mg/dl vs. 13.96 ± 7.09 mg/dl).

    Mean BMI in patients with diabetes was higher (27.06 ± 3.60) than mean BMI in healthy patient (22.47 ± 0.99). Therefore, compared to healthy patients, diabetic patients have statistically significant higher levels of salivary glucose, average plasma glucose, and BMI.

    Gender Base Analysis

    Sex-gender differences are fundamental also in both type 1 and type 2 diabetes. Just for example in the prediabetes situation impaired fasting glucose (expression of increased insulin resistance) is more common in men.

    while impaired glucose tolerance (expression of beta cell deficiency) is more common in female, indicating a possible different genesis of type 2 diabetes in the two sexes. In type 1 diabetes male and female are equivalent as incidence of the disease since puberty, while estrogens act as protective and reduce the incidence of type 1 diabetes in female after puberty.
